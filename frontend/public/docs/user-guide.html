User Guide

T1 Chiller Plant Digital Twin — How to Use

A step-by-step guide to operating the web app: navigating the 2D and 3D plant views, changing setpoints, reading efficiency, and understanding how the model tracks real plant data. No prior training required — everything happens in the browser.

1 Opening the app

The app runs in any modern browser. If it is already hosted for you, just open the link. To run it locally:

  1. Open a terminal in the project's frontend folder.
  2. Run npm install once, then npm run dev.
  3. Open the address it prints in your browser.

Good to know

The twin is a self-contained physics model — it does not need a connection to the live plant. You can explore freely; nothing you change here affects any real equipment.

3 Moving around

  • Switch system — use the three tabs in the top bar. This guide covers the Chiller Plant; Heat Exchanger and AHU work similarly.
  • Hide / show the left panel — the ☰ button at the top-left.
  • Hide / show the right panel — the ⋮ button at the top-right (handy for a bigger plant view).
  • Switch the left panel's content — the two icons on the thin rail: Assets (boxes icon) and Virtual Simulator (building icon).
  • Switch 2D ↔ 3D — the ⬢ 3D / ◱ 2D button beside Σ Summary at the top of the plant view.

4 The 2D plant diagram

The default view is the whole plant drawn to match the real T1 layout: 5 chillers, 6 chilled-water pumps, 6 condenser-water pumps and 5 cooling towers.

  • Flow arrows on the pipes show the direction water is moving — chilled water to the building, condenser water to the towers.
  • Faceplate labels on each machine show its key live readings (power, temperatures, status), just like a BMS graphic.
  • Click any equipment to select it; it is also highlighted in the left-panel asset list, and vice-versa.
  • Scroll to zoom · drag to pan — the + / − / ⊡ buttons at the top-right do the same.

5 The 3D plant view

Click ⬢ 3D (beside Σ Summary) to walk the plant room in 3D. Every machine is modelled on its real-world counterpart — centrifugal chillers with bolted tube-sheet covers, two-cell crossflow cooling towers with spinning fans, end-suction pump sets, pressure tanks and bypass valves — and every unit carries a live BMS data tile.

6 Changing setpoints — the Controls tab

Open the right panel's Controls tab. Settings are grouped into boxes that mirror a real SCADA screen: Outdoor Condition, CHWP / CWP / CT Control, Cooling Load, Heat Rejected, Heat Balance, and CHW Riser load shares.

How to change a value

  1. Editable settings appear as input boxes; read-only sensor values are plain text.
  2. Click the box, type a value, then press Enter or click away to apply. Press Esc to cancel.
  3. The whole plant recomputes instantly — power, temperatures, flows and efficiency all update to the new steady state, in both 2D and 3D.

Values are automatically kept within each machine's safe operating range.

Watch the calibration badge

At the top of the Controls tab a badge shows whether your inputs are inside the range the model was validated against: ● Calibrated means the result is data-backed; ▲ Extrapolating means you've gone outside the real data (e.g. a very high load or an unusual setpoint) and the numbers are a physics estimate — it lists exactly which inputs are out of range. Trust calibrated results; treat extrapolated ones as indicative.

7 The BMS Points tab

This tab lists every sensor point in the dataset with the twin's current value — the same point names as the real BMS. It also holds two interactive tools.

Duty chips — choose which units run

At the top, each category (CH, CHWP, CWP, CT) shows numbered chips. A highlighted chip is on duty (running); click it to send that unit to standby, and another unit takes its place.

Note

How many units run is decided automatically by the cooling load. The chips only choose which specific units serve that count — exactly like duty rotation on the real plant.

Inline edits

Two points marked with a pencil ✎ — the cooling load (rt) and the chilled-water supply setpoint (Header-hcwst) — can be typed directly here, the same way as on the Controls tab.

8 Efficiency & the before→after effect

Switch the left panel to Virtual Simulator (the building icon on the rail). It shows the impact of whatever you last changed.

  • Performance & efficiency — the headline numbers: building load, Plant kW/RT (the efficiency metric), total plant kW, Plant COP, CHW ΔT, tower approach, header DP, and units online.
  • Before → after — when you apply a change, each affected figure shows its previous value beside the new one, so you can see the knock-on ("domino") effect of your setpoint move.
  • Parameters affected — a short note on what your last input drives.

The number that matters

Plant kW/RT is the plant's efficiency — kilowatts of electricity per ton of cooling. Lower is better. Your goal when tuning setpoints is to reduce it without tripping any alarm.

9 How the twin tracks real data

The model is calibrated to a month of real 1-minute BMS trend data from the T1 plant and validated by blind replay: feeding it only the measured operator inputs of real minutes and comparing everything it computes against the recorded readings.

  • Across the full validation window it reproduces the plant's measured efficiency with an average error of 0.28% — which is the plant's own minute-to-minute noise level, the best any model can achieve.
  • The remaining small differences are real sensor noise and control hunting, not model error: the plant itself records slightly different power at identical settings.
  • The full validation (per-point replay of 133 real minutes) can be rerun by the engineering team at any time via the bundled scripts (validateRow86, validateMvWindow). An in-app row-replay comparison view also exists and can be enabled for engineering builds.

11 Quick answers

  • I changed a value — how do I undo it? Re-type the original value, or reload the page — everything resets to the plant's reference operating point.
  • Why did my typed number change slightly? Values are clamped to the equipment's safe range; if you exceed a limit it snaps to the nearest allowed value.
  • Nothing seems to animate. That's intended for the process — this is a steady-state model, so each change jumps straight to the new stable operating point. (The 3D tower fans do spin, purely as a running/standby indicator.)
  • The 3D view feels slow. It uses your GPU via WebGL; close other heavy tabs, or switch back to ◱ 2D — both views drive the identical model.
  • A value looks wrong / off. Check the calibration badge — if it says ▲ Extrapolating, you're outside the validated range and the figure is a physics estimate.
